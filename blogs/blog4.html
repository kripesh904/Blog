Nepal, Hidden by the Shadows of Discrimination

Kripesh Raj Sharma

Nepal is a country of vivid and distinct culture and traditions, but sadly, discrimination lies within the culture of Nepalese society. Discrimination is not a new-age concept for human society, but societies have been adopting the concept of inclusiveness. Regardless, such problems still exist on different levels. In Nepal, caste discrimination hasn’t come to a halt due to stereotyping of castes, untouchability, racist/casteist slurs, and discriminated opportunities. Thus, Nepal, a beautiful country, has been covered by the shadows of discrimination.

The major issue with Nepalese rural as well as some urban societies is untouchability. The so-called lower-caste people are suppressed and subjected to disgust, so much so that their mere touch disgraces the frail superiority of the so-called upper castes. This is a problem so common in the rural setup in Nepal, but the only solutions yet explored are quotas and reservations.

Another major issue lies in the form of racial/casteist stereotyping. This rarely comes up as a major issue, but it infuses discrimination in the blood of an average working man. This is an example of how minor-looking comments, in the façade of a joke or laughter, helps prove and perpetuates the long-lasting problems of the state.

Similarly, another issue is that of racial/casteist slurs. A comment meant to degrade and ridicule another race or caste. Such slurs hamper the confidence and aspirations of a person from a similar environment and lifestyle, just another race. This contributes to the overall problem of racial discrimination and only cements its foundation for such social evils.

Finally, another major issue is that of varied opportunities, discrimination that we see in real life. Whether it is getting a home to rent or job opportunities in small to big corporations, if you ask any so-called discriminated races, their search for a home to rent is just a long-living, never-ending case of discrimination. Such cases have been highlighted in the media but have led to no solution.

To sum it up, racial and caste discrimination has thrived in Nepalese society as long as the society itself. It may be as minor-seeming as stereotyping or as major as untouchability. The Nepalese society still has a major mark to remove for societal development and maturity. Even though I personally haven't faced it, a problem does exist, and it should be solved right now and the solution starts from us; not the government, not any organizations; the change will begin only from us, within us.
